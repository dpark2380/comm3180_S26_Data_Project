# Research Questions and Hypotheses

## Project Concept

Existing urban liveability indices, such as the EIU Global Liveability Index and the Mercer Quality of Living ranking, are designed for wealthy expatriates and corporations making relocation decisions. They rank cities highly based on quality of services and infrastructure without any regard for what it actually costs to live there. As a result, their top-ranked cities are almost exclusively in wealthy Western Europe and the Anglosphere.

This project builds an original "value for living" index that treats quality and cost as separate dimensions. Quality is measured using a composite of Numbeo city-level sub-indices (Healthcare, Crime, Pollution, Traffic), and cost is measured using Numbeo's Cost of Living Index. Dividing quality by cost produces a value ratio that reveals cities which offer a high standard of living at an affordable price.

---

## Research Questions and Hypotheses

### RQ1: Which cities score highest on quality of life relative to cost of living?

**Hypothesis:** The highest-value cities will not be the usual top-ranked liveability cities (Vienna, Copenhagen, Zurich). Instead, they will be mid-sized cities in Eastern Europe, Southeast Asia, and Latin America, where public services are strong relative to the local cost base.

---

### RQ2: Are sweet spot cities clustered in particular world regions, and does national wealth explain the pattern?

**Hypothesis:** Sweet spot cities will cluster in middle-income countries (roughly $10,000 to $30,000 GDP per capita PPP). Very low-income countries will score poorly on quality despite low costs, and very high-income countries will score well on quality but have costs that outpace it. The relationship between national wealth and value will follow an inverted U-shape.

---

### RQ3: Which dimension (health, crime, pollution, or traffic) most strongly drives overall quality of life scores?

**Hypothesis:** Healthcare will be the strongest driver of overall quality scores, as it has the widest variance across cities and is the dimension most directly shaped by national investment decisions. Crime will be the second most influential, particularly in pulling down scores for otherwise affordable cities in Latin America and Southern Africa.

---

### RQ4: Does income inequality explain why some low-cost cities still score poorly on quality of life?

**Hypothesis:** Cities in high-inequality countries (high Gini index) will score below what their cost of living would predict. Inequality concentrates quality of life gains among wealthy residents, while crowd-sourced indices like Numbeo reflect the experience of the connected, urban middle class rather than the full population.

---

### RQ5: Has the cost of living risen faster than quality of life since COVID?

**Hypothesis:** Cost of living has risen faster than quality of life since 2020, meaning the value index has declined on average across cities. The effect will be most pronounced in cities that saw large post-COVID demand surges, particularly in Southern Europe, Southeast Asia, and major Anglophone cities.
